Instructions for use of this factorizer:

Return to the Math APIs page.

  • This page allows for four different types of calculations, depending upon the following pairs of circumstances:
    • real integers vs complex integers" (AKA Gaussian integers)
    • single integer (factorization thereof) vs list of integers (greatest common divisor - GCD - thereof)
  • URL:
    • After ...herokuapp.com above you should type either /complex/ if you want to consider Gaussian integers or nothing if you want to consider real ones.
    • After this you should type json/ if you want the output in json or a single slash if you want the output in html.
    • After this you should type your input, as described next.
  • Input(s):
    • Spaces are allowed - but discouraged - because %20 will replace each space after you hit return, thereby making the address uglier.
    • E-notation is not allowed.
    • All real numbers must be integers less than 263 in magnitude (approximately "9" followed by 18 zeros). Exception: the limit on the number of digits in your Gaussian integer is half of this.
    • Complex numbers should be of the form m + ni, where m and n are integers as described in the previous bullet. Standard shortcuts are allowed, such as shortening 3 + 1i to 3 + i, or shortening 0 + 2i to 2i.
    • Use commas to separate numbers in a list, ie when determining GCD.
  • Factorization theorems:
    • real factorization:
      Any nonzero (real) integer may be expressed uniquely as a product of ±1 and zero or more prime numbers (e.g., 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, etc), each raised to a power of 1 or more.
    • complex factorization:
      Any nonzero Gaussian integer may be expressed as a product of either ±1 or ±i, and zero or more Gaussian primes, each raised to a power of 1 or more. This expression is unique if the Gaussian primes are restricted to the first quadrant of the complex plane, including the positive real axis but not including the positive imaginary axis in the complex plane.
    • Whether real or complex, any nonzero integer other than ±1 or ±i is categorized as one of the following:
      • Prime number contains exactly one prime factor in its factorization.
      • Composite number contains two or more prime factors in its factorization. Although this definition usually is used for positive integers, it extends naturally to Gaussign integers.
    • Just as individual integers may be classified as either prime or composite, so may lists of integers be classified by their GCD: the list is relatively prime (or "coprime" or "mutually prime") if their GCD equals 1, or relatively composite (or "mutually composite") if their GCD is larger than 1.
  • json output:
    The factors property of the json output will be a POJO/dictionary, each key for which is a different prime number, and each value for which is the power to which the particular prime is raised when forming the factorization. For complex integers, the value of the exponent property is the power to which i should be raised when multiplying the prime factors,
